I like reading very much, and this habit of reading has been cultivated since
childhood. My parents are both university professors, and their
lives are very simple; in addition to teaching, they also do research, and that’s
about it. So weekends in our family usually involve sitting on the sofa and
reading books together, unless of course we go to the library or a bookstore. My
parents read their academic books, and sometimes grade papers or write
articles, while I read a variety of material. When I was younger and illiterate(N

in various types of books over the years.
When I was young, my parents gave me nothing but books, and every time I
received one, I was so happy that I couldn’t wait to read it.
Reading is a habit of mine and the most important thing in my life. Even though
I don't have that much free time now, due to work, I try to read for an hour
every night before going to bed. On weekends, I usually choose to stay at home
unless required to attend some necessary social activity.
When the weather is good, I make a cup of tea and sit on the balcony and read.
When the weather is bad, I lie on my reclining chair or bed and read. I

Books are the air I breathe, and I cannot live without them.

### Part 3 Questions

**1. Why are children attracted to new things (such as electronics)?**

**A:** Well, I believe children are naturally curious, and new things like electronics offer endless opportunities
to explore and learn. From my perspective as a woman who loves technology, I think gadgets captivate
kids because they combine fun with education, making learning enjoyable. Moreover, the bright colors,
sounds, and interactive features of these devices are highly engaging for young minds. Children often
imitate adults using these devices, which also fuels their interest. In essence, the appeal lies in both the
novelty and the instant gratification these electronics provide.

**2. Why do some grown-ups hate to throw out old things (such as clothes)?**

**A:** Certainly! Here's a unique answer from the perspective of a male speaker:.
Many adults find it hard to part with old belongings, like clothes, because these items often carry
sentimental value. For instance, a worn-out jacket might remind them of a memorable trip or an important
phase in life. Besides emotions, practicality plays a role; some believe that old clothes can be reused or
repurposed later. Additionally, in today’s throwaway culture, some grown-ups resist wastefulness by
holding onto things as long as possible, viewing it as a way to save money and reduce environmental
impact. Thus, both memories and mindset contribute to this reluctance.

**3. Is the way people buy things affected? How?**

**A:** Of course it is. People are social animals. We live in groups and we communicate with others on a daily
basis. People exchange information and opinions on all sorts of things, including shopping. And these
thoughts and opinions are taken seriously. For example, many young people have started to shop online.
The young people’s parents are catching up with this trend as well. Even children know it’s wise to check
out toy types and prices online before they buy them. So yeah, it is inevitable for people to be influenced
by each other.

**4. What do you think influences people to buy new things?**

**A:** For one, people are often influenced by advertisements when they make shopping decisions. Advertisers
know only too well how to manipulate consumers. They use articulate language to arouse consumers and
stimulate their desire to buy the advertised products. Also, many people listen to their family or
friends’opinions when shopping. If someone close to them gives them positive feedback on certain
products, they are likely to trust these products too and go ahead and buy them. For example, while there
are thousands of shampoo brands, we’d be thankful if someone we trust tells us which one is of good
quality.

**5. Why do all children like toys?**

**A:** Children are naturally drawn to toys because these objects spark their imagination and creativity. For
example, a simple toy car can become a spaceship or a racing vehicle in a child's mind, allowing them to
explore different scenarios. Moreover, toys help kids develop important skills, such as hand-eye
coordination and problem-solving. From a social perspective, playing with toys often encourages
interaction with friends or family, making it a joyful experience. Ultimately, toys serve as both
entertainment and learning tools, which is why almost every child is fond of them.
Children are naturally drawn to toys because they spark imagination and creativity. Toys provide a way
for kids to explore the world around them, allowing them to learn new skills while having fun. Moreover,
toys often serve as comfort objects, offering a sense of security. For instance, many children form strong
attachments to stuffed animals or action figures, which help them feel safe and entertained. Additionally,
toys encourage social interaction when kids play together, fostering friendships and communication. In
essence, toys fulfill both educational and emotional needs, which is why they hold such universal appeal
among children.

**6. Do you think it is good for a child to always take his or her favourite toy with them all the time?**

**A:** Carrying a favorite toy everywhere can provide comfort and a sense of security for a child, especially in
unfamiliar situations. However, relying on the toy constantly might hinder the child's ability to adapt and
face challenges independently. For instance, if a child depends too much on their toy, they may feel
anxious when it’s not available. On the other hand, having a beloved item can also encourage imaginative
play and creativity. Ultimately, a balance is important; occasional use of a favorite toy is beneficial, but
children should also learn to explore and develop resilience without it.

The shopping mall I visit most frequently is called "MixC World," situated right
next to my university campus. Given its proximity, I pop in quite often—easily
three or four times a week, not always to shop but sometimes just to wander
around or meet friends.
What makes MixC World special is its blend of practicality and trendy appeal.
The basement level houses a well-stocked supermarket where I grab daily
necessities like snacks, toiletries, and sometimes ingredients for a simple dorm-
cooked meal. The ground and first floors are lined with popular youth brands
like Li-Ning and Huawei, where I've purchased sportswear for gym sessions
and electronic accessories. The upper floors, however, are the real highlight for
students: a massive food court offering affordable meals from various cuisines
and a co-working space with free Wi-Fi where I often study with classmates.
Beyond shopping, this mall functions as a social hub for us. I feel it’s designed
with young consumers in mind, offering both what we need and spaces where
we want to spend time. It’s more than a commercial center; it’s an extension of
our campus life.

### Part 3 Questions

**0. What kind of people like shopping?**

**A:** I believe that people who enjoy shopping often have a keen eye for fashion or enjoy the social aspect of it.
For instance, my close friends love browsing through boutiques not just to buy clothes, but also to stay
updated on the latest trends. Additionally, some folks shop as a way to unwind after a stressful day; for
them, it’s almost therapeutic. Personally, I appreciate shopping when I need something specific, but I
understand why many find it exciting and even fun.

**1. Why are some people keen on shopping?**

**A:** Many individuals enjoy shopping because it offers a form of escapism from their daily routines. For
some, visiting stores or browsing online provides a chance to discover new trends and express their
personal style. Additionally, shopping can be a social activity where people bond with friends or family.
Beyond that, the thrill of finding a good deal or a rare item gives a sense of achievement. Therefore,
shopping is not just about acquiring goods but also about the experience and emotional satisfaction it
brings.

**2. Do you thinkit's a waste of time to go shopping?**

**A:** Personally, I don't believe shopping is a waste of time. In fact, it can be quite enjoyable and useful. For
instance, when I shop, I get a chance to explore new products and compare prices, which helps me make
better decisions. Moreover, shopping can be a social activity; sometimes, I go with friends and we catch
up while browsing stores. On the other hand, online shopping has saved me a lot of time, allowing me to
focus on other important tasks. So, rather than being a waste, shopping can be both productive and
relaxing depending on how you approach it.

**3. Why do people buy things they don't need?**

**A:** People often purchase items they don’t truly need for various reasons. Sometimes, it’s driven by
emotional factors like stress or boredom, where shopping acts as a temporary escape. Additionally,
advertising can be very persuasive, making products seem essential when they aren’t. Social influence
also plays a role; people may buy things to fit in or impress others. Moreover, the convenience of online
shopping can lead to impulsive buys. Overall, these purchases reveal more about people’s desires and
social pressures than practical necessities.

- How he/she encouraged you
- What he/she encouraged you to do

### Part 2 Sample Answer

Professor Zhao, who taught my elective course “Urban Ecology,”
fundamentally reshaped my understanding of nature protection in a Chinese
context. During a field trip to a city wetland park, he didn’t just identify plants;
he analyzed how this engineered ecosystem reduced the urban heat island effect
and processed stormwater runoff, calling it “green infrastructure.”.
His encouragement was intellectual and actionable. He assigned a group project
to audit our university campus’s biodiversity and resource flows. Under his
guidance, we mapped wasteful energy use in older buildings and proposed low-
cost retrofit solutions to the administration. He emphasized that in densely
populated China, protecting nature isn’t about leaving it untouched, but about
intelligently designing human systems to work with ecological processes.
This led me to volunteer for a local NGO monitoring air quality data. I feel
immense respect for Professor Zhao’s ability to bridge theory and practice. He
equipped me with a systemic lens to see cities as living organisms and myself
as an agent within them, capable of contributing to ecological resilience through
informed choices and advocacy.

### Part 3 Questions

**0. Do you think there should be laws to protect nature?**

**A:** Absolutely, I believe legislation is essential to safeguard the environment. Without legal frameworks,
efforts to conserve nature would likely be inconsistent and ineffective. For instance, laws can regulate
pollution levels, protect endangered species, and preserve natural habitats. Apart from that, such
regulations encourage industries and individuals to adopt sustainable practices. In my opinion, beyond
moral obligations, enforced laws provide a structured and reliable means of ensuring that future
generations inherit a healthier planet. Therefore, having environmental protection laws is not just
beneficial but necessary in today’s world.

**1. Should schools teach children to get close to nature?**

**A:** Absolutely, I believe schools should encourage children to connect with nature. Growing up in a bustling
city, I rarely had the chance to explore natural environments, which made me appreciate green spaces
even more as I got older. Experiencing nature firsthand fosters creativity and empathy, which technology
alone can’t provide. For instance, outdoor lessons can teach kids about ecosystems and responsibility
towards the planet. This approach not only enriches their knowledge but also promotes mental well-being,
which is especially important in today’s fast-paced world. Overall, nature education is vital for holistic
development.

**2. How can parents teach their children to protect nature?**

**A:** Parents play a crucial role in fostering environmental awareness in their children. They can start by
involving kids in outdoor activities like planting trees or cleaning local parks, which makes learning
hands-on and memorable. Additionally, explaining the impact of pollution and waste through stories or
documentaries helps children understand the bigger picture. By leading through example, such as
reducing plastic usage at home, parents instill sustainable habits early on. Ultimately, combining practical
experiences with thoughtful discussions can ignite a lifelong respect for nature in young minds.

**0. What are the differences between making friends in real life and online?**

reduced their frustration. The free samples also increased sales of the new cold
brew product.

rather than just the operational issue. It cost almost nothing but created
significant improvement by understanding customer psychology—people can
tolerate waits better when they feel informed and valued.
